# Phase 4: Cross-Case Comparison

status: completed
phase_goal: Compare representative AI use cases across business functions to identify common success conditions and recurring failure risks.

## Cases Included

1. Netflix personalization and recommendation systems
2. Upstart AI lending and credit underwriting
3. UPS ORION route optimization
4. Amazon experimental AI recruiting tool

## Comparison Table

| Case | Function | Decision Structure | Main Value Claim | Main Risk | Oversight Need | Overall Lesson |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Netflix | Marketing | high-volume, data-rich, repeated | better discovery and personalization | short-term optimization, system complexity | medium | AI works well in recommendation-heavy environments with abundant behavioral data |
| Upstart | Finance | high-impact, regulated, model-based | more efficient underwriting and broader credit evaluation | fairness, compliance, model change risk | high | value and governance pressure increase together |
| UPS ORION | Operations | structured, repeated, optimization-driven | route efficiency and service improvement | data brittleness and exception handling | medium | AI is strong where objectives are measurable and workflows are stable |
| Amazon hiring | Human resources | partially structured but socially sensitive | faster screening at scale | bias, legitimacy loss, weak trust | high | sensitive people decisions can fail even before full deployment |

## Cross-Case Findings

### Finding 1: AI performs best in structured decision environments with clear feedback loops

Netflix and UPS show that AI is especially effective when decisions are repeated, measurable, and supported by large amounts of operational or behavioral data.

### Finding 2: High-stakes decisions require more than predictive accuracy

Upstart and Amazon show that when decisions affect fairness, opportunity, or compliance, governance and oversight become essential determinants of success.

### Finding 3: The same capability can create value or failure depending on context design

Prediction, ranking, and optimization are not inherently beneficial. Their effect depends on training data, objective choice, workflow integration, and the ability of humans to intervene appropriately.

### Finding 4: Human involvement does not disappear in successful AI systems

Across all four cases, humans remain necessary for objective setting, exception handling, legal accountability, and interpretation of results.

## Implications for Phase 5

The analytical framework should evaluate AI adoption using at least six dimensions:

1. Decision structure
2. Data quality
3. Stakes and external impact
4. Degree of model autonomy
5. Human oversight design
6. Governance and accountability requirement
