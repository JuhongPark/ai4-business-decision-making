# Phase 2: Core Literature Review Summary

status: completed
phase_goal: Review the core literature needed to explain how AI affects business decision-making and what conditions shape performance outcomes.

## Review Scope

This phase focused on six foundational sources that collectively cover:

1. AI as prediction and decision support
2. Human-AI complementarity
3. Data-driven performance effects
4. Behavioral barriers to algorithm use
5. Governance and accountability

## Main Themes

### Theme 1: AI changes the economics of decision-making by lowering the cost of prediction

Agrawal, Gans, and Goldfarb argue that AI should be understood primarily as a prediction technology. This matters because many business decisions rely on predicting unknown states, but prediction alone does not produce action. Firms still need judgment to interpret payoffs, evaluate tradeoffs, and decide what should be done in context. This theme supports the study's decision to analyze AI as part of a broader organizational decision system rather than as a standalone technical capability.

### Theme 2: Better decision performance depends on organizational use of data, not only on model quality

Brynjolfsson, Hitt, and Kim show that data-driven decision-making is associated with stronger firm performance across productivity and related outcome measures. For this study, the implication is direct: AI adoption should not be treated as automatically performance-enhancing. Instead, the research should examine whether firms have the practices, data quality, and operating discipline required to turn analytical outputs into better decisions.

### Theme 3: Human-AI complementarity is stronger than pure substitution in complex organizations

Jarrahi argues that AI and humans bring different strengths to organizational decision-making. AI supports analytical processing and pattern recognition in complex environments, while humans contribute intuition, contextual interpretation, and sense-making under uncertainty and ambiguity. This supports a central proposition of the study: firms are likely to gain more value from augmentation than from full decision replacement.

### Theme 4: Trust problems can reduce decision quality even when algorithmic tools are capable

Dietvorst, Simmons, and Massey show that people can avoid algorithms after observing them make mistakes, even when the algorithms remain more reliable than human judgment. This is highly relevant for business settings because it means adoption failure may reflect behavioral distrust rather than technical weakness alone. The study should therefore examine both algorithm aversion and the opposite risk of excessive reliance.

### Theme 5: Governance is part of decision quality, not a separate compliance issue

NIST and OECD frame trustworthy AI around risk management, accountability, transparency, human oversight, and responsible use. These ideas are not peripheral to business decision-making. They determine whether AI systems can be integrated into high-stakes workflows in ways that are reliable, explainable, and defensible. Governance should therefore appear inside the analytical framework, not only in the conclusion.

## Synthesis

The literature suggests that the central research question should not be whether AI is useful in business decision-making in general. That framing is too broad and too weak. A stronger framing is to ask what factors determine whether AI-supported decisions improve organizational performance. The reviewed sources point to five likely explanatory factors:

1. The type of decision being supported
2. The quality and availability of relevant data
3. The degree of human judgment required
4. The behavioral trust relationship between managers and AI outputs
5. The presence of governance and accountability mechanisms

## Implications for the Study

- The literature review chapter should be organized by concepts, not by source chronology.
- The case analysis should compare not only business outcomes but also decision design and oversight design.
- The final framework should evaluate AI as an organizational capability embedded in workflows, incentives, and governance structures.

## Direction for Phase 3

The next step is to map AI use across major business functions and identify where the decision environment is most structured, most data-rich, and most sensitive to oversight requirements.
