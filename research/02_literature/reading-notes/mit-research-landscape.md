## Summary

MIT's most relevant research line for this project does not argue for uniform automation. Across collective intelligence, digital economy, hiring, and organizational adoption studies, the recurring finding is that AI creates value conditionally. The strongest MIT evidence supports adaptive authority, workflow-sensitive oversight, and task-specific allocation between humans and AI.

## Key Findings

- The MIT collective intelligence line shows that human-AI combinations are not automatically superior. They help more often in creative work than in decision tasks, which supports conservative authority assignment in high-stakes domains.
- The MIT digital economy line shows that performance gains depend on organizational complements such as data quality, workflow fit, and worker learning.
- The MIT hiring line shows that algorithm design is a governance choice. Different search and ranking logic can change diversity and candidate quality simultaneously.
- The MIT organizational adoption line shows that employees do not respond to AI systems mechanically. They may over-intervene, under-trust, or reshape work around the system in ways that alter realized performance.

## Relevance to Research Question

This MIT evidence strengthens the study's central claim that AI authority should be adaptive rather than fixed. It also supports broadening the literature review from model capability to institutional design. The strongest MIT contribution is not a general claim that AI improves decisions. It is the more defensible claim that outcomes depend on task type, organizational complements, trust calibration, and governance design.

## Leadership Note

Current organizational leadership and study leadership are not always the same. As of March 9, 2026, the MIT Center for Collective Intelligence is directed by Thomas Malone, the MIT Initiative on the Digital Economy is directed by Sinan Aral, and the MIT Institute for Work and Employment Research is co-directed by Erin Kelly. The study-specific researchers most relevant to this project are Malone, Almaatouq, Vaccaro, Li, Raymond, Bergman, and Kellogg.

## My Notes

This synthesis is useful as a dedicated literature subsection and as a bridge between the conceptual review and the case-based governance framework. It is also a good source base for explaining why MIT research does not support blanket automation claims in finance, hiring, or other high-stakes domains.

MIT SDM should be treated as a complementary stream rather than folded into the Sloan-centered literature. Its value is strongest on implementation design, sociotechnical systems, and workflow-level framing.
