## Strengths

- The framework correctly treats governance as part of decision quality.
- High-stakes domains receive more caution than low-risk operational domains.
- The draft recognizes fairness, explainability, and accountability as central issues.

## Findings

### Finding 1: The Amazon hiring case is evidentially weaker than the regulated cases

The Amazon hiring example is widely cited, but the draft currently relies on secondary reporting rather than official company disclosure or regulator documentation. That does not make the case unusable, but the paper should label it as a reported case and avoid overstating internal facts as fully verified.

### Finding 2: Company-reported efficiency claims need qualification

UPS and Netflix are strong examples of structured AI deployment, but many reported benefits come from company communications. These are useful for understanding intended value and workflow design, yet they are not equivalent to independent audit evidence.

### Finding 3: Final judgment should separate low-risk automation from high-impact decisions more sharply

The framework already moves in this direction, but the final paper should make the default presumption explicit: high-impact domains require stronger human oversight and stronger governance before any substantial automation is considered.

## Required Revisions

1. Reframe the Amazon case as Reuters-reported and exploratory
2. Clarify when outcomes are company-reported versus regulator-supported
3. State an explicit default caution for finance and HR use cases

## Verdict

The draft is close to defensible, but only if its highest-risk claims are narrowed and evidence quality is labeled more precisely.
