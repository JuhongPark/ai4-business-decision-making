## Strengths

- The paper maintains a coherent central research question.
- The literature review establishes a clear conceptual distinction between prediction and judgment.
- The argument that AI performance is conditional rather than universal is logically consistent across sections.

## Findings

### Finding 1: Some claims need tighter causal language

The draft sometimes implies that certain business outcomes follow from AI adoption when the supporting evidence is largely conceptual, correlational, or company-reported. Phrases such as "AI can improve business decision-making" are acceptable if kept conditional, but claims should avoid sounding experimentally established across all domains.

### Finding 2: Evidence quality varies across the case set

The case section combines official company materials, regulator statements, and externally reported press coverage. This is acceptable for exploratory business research, but the draft should explicitly state that case evidence is uneven and not equally strong across all examples.

### Finding 3: Methodological limitations should be stated more clearly

The paper is built on secondary sources, conceptual synthesis, and public case materials. That is a valid design, but the limitations should be explicit in the final paper rather than only implied.

## Required Revisions

1. Tighten causal phrasing in the introduction, benefits, and conclusion
2. Add a short limitations paragraph explaining the uneven quality of public case evidence
3. Distinguish regulator-supported and company-reported evidence more clearly in the case section

## Verdict

The paper is conceptually defensible, but it should be framed as a structured qualitative analysis rather than as a broadly established causal demonstration.
