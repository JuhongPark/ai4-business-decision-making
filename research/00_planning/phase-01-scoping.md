# Phase 1: Research Problem and Scope Definition

status: completed
phase_goal: Define what this study will investigate, why the topic matters, and how the research boundaries should be set before the literature review begins.

## Research Problem

AI is increasingly embedded in business decision processes through forecasting, recommendation, optimization, and risk assessment systems. However, business value does not come from prediction quality alone. Early scoping research shows that decision quality depends on the interaction between AI outputs, managerial judgment, data quality, and governance. This creates a practical research problem: firms are adopting AI for decision support, but the conditions under which AI actually improves organizational performance remain uneven and poorly specified.

## Why This Problem Matters

- AI lowers the cost of prediction, which changes how firms structure decisions under uncertainty.
- Data-driven decision-making is associated with stronger firm performance, which makes AI adoption strategically important.
- Organizations still face algorithm aversion, overreliance, opacity, and accountability problems.
- Governance and human oversight remain necessary even when AI systems perform well on narrow tasks.

## Scope Definition

unit_of_analysis: firm-level business decision processes
primary_focus: AI as a decision-support capability in organizational settings
decision_domains_in_scope:
- strategy support
- marketing and customer analytics
- finance and risk assessment
- operations and supply chain management
- human resources decision support

out_of_scope:
- low-level model engineering
- public-sector policy design
- consumer-facing AI behavior outside business decision processes
- fully technical benchmarking of model architectures

## Refined Research Question

main_question: What factors determine whether AI-supported business decisions lead to better organizational performance?

## Supporting Questions

1. In which business functions is AI most commonly used for decision support?
2. What kinds of decisions benefit most from AI tools?
3. What organizational and technical factors influence the quality of AI-supported decisions?
4. What risks arise when firms rely on AI in decision processes?
5. How should firms balance AI recommendations with human judgment?

## Working Argument

AI improves business decision-making most effectively when it augments rather than replaces managerial judgment, relies on strong data quality, and operates within clear governance and accountability structures.

## Phase 1 Findings

### Finding 1: AI should be framed as part of a broader decision system

Scoping sources consistently indicate that AI is not valuable as an isolated model. Its value depends on how prediction, judgment, action, and accountability are connected inside the organization.

### Finding 2: Human judgment remains central in complex business environments

AI is strongest in pattern recognition and prediction, but organizations still need human interpretation when decisions involve ambiguity, tradeoffs, or contextual uncertainty.

### Finding 3: Data quality and organizational readiness are likely core explanatory variables

The research should not treat AI adoption as a binary condition. A better approach is to examine readiness factors such as data quality, workflow integration, oversight design, and decision rights.

### Finding 4: Trust problems operate in both directions

Businesses can underuse AI because managers distrust algorithmic outputs, or overuse AI because they defer to them too easily. The study therefore needs to evaluate both algorithm aversion and overreliance.

### Finding 5: Governance must be part of the research framework from the start

Trustworthiness, traceability, and accountability are not side issues. They shape whether AI can be used responsibly in high-impact business decisions.

## Analytical Direction for Phase 2

The literature review should now be organized around five dimensions:

1. AI as prediction and decision support
2. Human-AI complementarity
3. Data-driven performance effects
4. Behavioral trust problems such as algorithm aversion
5. Governance, risk, and accountability

## Sources Used in Phase 1

1. Agrawal, Gans, and Goldfarb on prediction, judgment, and complexity
2. Brynjolfsson, Hitt, and Kim on data-driven decision-making and firm performance
3. Jarrahi on human-AI symbiosis in organizational decision-making
4. Dietvorst, Simmons, and Massey on algorithm aversion
5. NIST AI RMF and OECD AI Principles on trustworthiness, risk, and accountability

## Phase 1 Output

This phase establishes the study as a business research project about conditions for effective AI-assisted decision-making, not a generic overview of AI in business.
