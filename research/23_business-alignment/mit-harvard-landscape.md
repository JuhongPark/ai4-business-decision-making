# MIT and Harvard Research Relevant to Business-Context AI Alignment

status: draft
date: 2026-03-21
purpose: Narrow the recent external scan to MIT- and Harvard-linked research most relevant to the repository's emerging focus on business-context AI alignment.
scope: 2024-2026 institution-linked and primary sources

## Scope Note

This note does not review all AI research at MIT or Harvard.

It focuses on the business-facing and organizational research most relevant to the following question:

**How should firms design, calibrate, and govern AI-supported decision systems so that delegation remains effective, trustworthy, risk-bounded, and organizationally accountable?**

That means the scan prioritizes:

- business-school and organization-facing research
- human-AI decision design
- trust calibration and AI aversion
- workflow and team redesign
- governance, risk, and accountability
- experimentation and operationalization in firms

## Main Takeaway

MIT and Harvard are not primarily using the phrase **AI alignment** in the business-facing work sampled here.

Instead, they are studying adjacent problems that are directly useful for a business-context alignment agenda:

- when human-AI combinations outperform either humans or AI alone
- when people trust or reject AI
- how AI changes team structure and expertise
- how AI rationales shape subjective judgment
- how organizations should map risk and assign accountability
- how firms should build the operating capability to deploy AI responsibly at scale

In other words, the most relevant MIT and Harvard work does not treat alignment as only a model-level property.

It treats the problem more as:

**workflow design, authority allocation, trust calibration, and governance under real organizational constraints**

## MIT: Strongest Themes

### 1. Human-AI complementarity depends on task design, not on a simple "more AI is better" logic

MIT Sloan and the MIT Center for Collective Intelligence have produced one of the clearest recent warnings against naive hybrid deployment.

Vaccaro, Almaatouq, and Malone's 2024 meta-analysis found that human-AI combinations often underperformed the better of human-only or AI-only systems on decision tasks, while creative tasks were more promising.

Why this matters:

- MIT is strongly reinforcing the idea that the business problem is not adoption alone
- the real question is how to divide labor between humans and AI
- this supports your existing repository logic around conditional delegation and authority calibration

### 2. MIT is making trust calibration partly a psychological-fit problem

Jackson Lu's 2025 work on AI aversion reframes adoption as a capability-personalization problem.

The core result is that people prefer AI when it is seen as more capable and when personalization is not seen as necessary.

Why this matters:

- this is highly relevant to business-context alignment because reliance depends on more than technical accuracy
- organizations need to understand where people will under-trust, over-trust, or reject AI for non-performance reasons
- it gives a more precise vocabulary for studying when delegated authority will be accepted or resisted

### 3. MIT is also emphasizing user capability, not just model capability

Another 2025 MIT Sloan study found that generative AI improved employee creativity only for workers with stronger metacognitive strategies.

The implication is that AI performance gains depend on the user's ability to monitor, revise, and strategically engage with the system.

Why this matters:

- this strengthens the argument that business-context alignment is partly a capability-design problem
- firms may need training, interface design, and oversight norms, not just better models
- it also connects to your interest in calibrated reliance rather than passive tool use

### 4. MIT's more organizational research is moving toward explicit governance and risk architecture

MIT CISR's January 15, 2026 research briefing on generative AI risk argues that firms should distinguish between embedded risks inherited from the technology and enacted risks created by organizational choices.

It recommends inventorying GenAI deployments, assigning accountability, and keeping audit trails for prompts, outputs, and human interventions.

Why this matters:

- this is one of the clearest MIT sources for operational governance
- it is very close to your repository's emphasis on fallback, monitoring, and authority review
- it also supports a business-context alignment frame where misuse and misgovernance are as important as raw model quality

### 5. MIT is building institute-level infrastructure around applied business deployment questions

Recent MIT-wide initiatives suggest that the institute sees AI as an organizational and societal deployment problem, not only a technical one.

The MIT Generative AI Impact Consortium explicitly asks:

- how AI-human collaboration can create outcomes that neither could achieve alone
- what the dynamic is between AI systems and human behavior
- how interdisciplinary research can guide better and safer AI

MIT's Work of the Future Initiative is similarly focused on case studies, benchmarking, training, and responsible use in real organizations.

Why this matters:

- MIT appears to be institutionalizing a research agenda around business deployment, work redesign, and responsible adoption
- this provides a strong ecosystem for work on delegation, guardrails, and trust calibration

## Harvard: Strongest Themes

### 1. Harvard is especially strong on field experiments about how AI reshapes collaborative work

Harvard Business School and D^3's 2025 "Cybernetic Teammate" work shows AI acting not just as a tool, but as a collaborator that changes team structure.

In a field experiment with 776 professionals at Procter & Gamble, individuals with AI matched the performance of teams without AI, while AI also reduced functional silos and improved emotional experience.

Why this matters:

- Harvard is producing strong evidence that AI changes how expertise is combined inside firms
- this pushes your research toward team design, not only individual decision support
- it also supports the idea that AI delegation can substitute for some functions of organizational coordination

### 2. Harvard is treating subjective evaluation as a governance problem

Jacqueline Lane and collaborators' 2025 work on narrative AI in innovation evaluation shows that AI recommendations with rationales can strongly shape human screening decisions, especially for subjective criteria.

The study finds that evaluators using AI were more likely to fail solutions, and that narrative AI was particularly influential when subjective judgment was involved.

Why this matters:

- this is directly relevant to business decisions involving screening, prioritization, and innovation selection
- it suggests that AI rationales should be treated as governance objects, not just explanation features
- this is a major contribution to your emerging idea of miscalibrated delegation

### 3. Harvard is also studying how AI structures decision processes, not just outcomes

The 2026 D^3 piece on the "mean-variance innovation tradeoff" argues that the sequence of AI-assisted evaluation changes what kind of innovation gets selected.

Feasibility-first and novelty-first screening produce different portfolio effects, and interactive chatbot formats can increase exploration but lower average quality.

Why this matters:

- Harvard is paying attention to path dependence in AI-supported decision systems
- this is very relevant to business-context alignment because the way AI is inserted into a process shapes what kinds of judgments become likely
- it expands the problem from "Is the recommendation good?" to "How does the AI change the structure of judgment over time?"

### 4. Harvard is investing in AI operationalization and experimental capability

The Data Science & AI Operations Lab explicitly studies how organizations integrate AI into operations for improved decision-making and automation.

Its agenda emphasizes experimentation, operationalization, trust, and redesigning operating models for scalable deployment.

The lab's personnel and listed research interests also show continued attention to:

- human-AI collaboration
- fairness, privacy, and transparency
- trust in LLMs
- optimal human-AI decision systems in domains such as hiring and loan approvals

Why this matters:

- Harvard is not only producing papers; it is building a durable operations-and-experimentation research platform
- this matches your interest in practically usable governance rather than abstract principles alone

### 5. Harvard provides a useful conceptual language for hybrid decision systems

Soroush Saghafian's 2024 work on the **human-algorithm centaur** argues that future AI development and use in many domains should focus more on strong human-algorithm combinations than on pure AI approaches.

Why this matters:

- this gives a direct conceptual bridge to your research
- it is useful for describing business-context alignment as the design of hybrid decision systems rather than the replacement of humans
- it also helps frame delegation as a design choice about where human intuition should remain active

## MIT vs Harvard: Comparative Reading

The clearest pattern from this scan is:

- MIT is strongest on conditions, guardrails, adoption psychology, risk mapping, and business governance architecture
- Harvard is strongest on field experiments, workflow configurations, AI interaction expertise, and how AI changes evaluation and collaboration inside firms

Inference from sources:

- MIT is closer to a **trust-calibration and governance-design** lens
- Harvard is closer to a **workflow-design and organizational-experimentation** lens

Both are highly relevant to your direction.

But they contribute different strengths:

- MIT helps explain **when AI authority should be granted, limited, or reviewed**
- Harvard helps explain **how AI should be inserted into real work systems and what new failure modes emerge when it is**

## What This Means For Your Research

The most natural synthesis is:

**Use MIT to frame the rule side of business-context AI alignment, and use Harvard to frame the workflow side.**

More concretely:

- MIT helps with trust calibration, authority thresholds, governance, accountability, and risk logic
- Harvard helps with interaction expertise, team redesign, subjective judgment, and decision-process sequencing

That combined frame is stronger than using either institution's work alone.

## Fit With The Current Repository

This repository already aligns well with much of the MIT/Harvard landscape.

Strong existing overlaps:

- authority allocation
- fallback and escalation
- adaptive governance
- risk-bounded delegation
- organizational accountability

The clearest areas where the repository could still grow are:

- a more explicit trust-calibration framework
- a concept of AI interaction expertise
- governance of AI rationales in subjective business decisions
- design logic for AI-assisted screening and sequencing
- a sharper account of how delegation reshapes team structure and expertise boundaries

## Directional Conclusion

If you continue this project as **business-context AI alignment**, MIT and Harvard together support the framing very well.

They suggest that the frontier for this kind of research is not:

- pure technical alignment

but rather:

- calibrated delegation
- workflow-specific trust
- decision-process design
- organizational governance
- bounded and auditable AI authority in firms

This is highly compatible with the direction already emerging in this repository.
