# Research Plan: Minimum Viable Governance for AI-Assisted Business Decisions

status: active
date_created: 2026-04-11
purpose: identify and execute research on the critical gaps between the 3-layer solution and deployable practice

## What We Know vs What We Don't

| We know (supported) | We don't know (gap) |
| --- | --- |
| "Own answer first" triples detection for single decisions | Does it work across a 6-stage pipeline? |
| Prompt design = 50% of output quality | Which specific bidirectional prompt structures reduce sycophancy for which task types? |
| Over-governance is counterproductive | What is the exact tipping point between too little and too much? |
| Shadow AI is the real baseline (90% unauthorized) | How does 3-layer solution integrate with existing shadow AI practices? |
| Absorptive capacity is the prerequisite | What is the minimum knowledge threshold, and can the task decomposition itself serve as the knowledge base? |
| Effective interventions are disliked by users (Buçinca 2021) | How to design interventions that work AND that people actually adopt? |
| Sycophancy compounds in pipelines (our experiment) | Nobody else has studied multi-stage compounding — this is our unique contribution |

## Five Research Tracks

### Track 1: Bidirectional Prompt Taxonomy
**Gap**: We know bidirectional prompts reduce sycophancy (Stage 4 recovery in our experiment), but we don't know which prompt structures work best for which task types.

**Research question**: What is the optimal bidirectional prompt structure for each of the 3 task profiles (prediction-dominant, interleaved, judgment-dominant)?

### Track 2: Pipeline-Level Cognitive Forcing
**Gap**: All cognitive forcing studies (Buçinca, Fogliato, de Jong, Vasconcelos, Swaroop) test SINGLE decisions. Our contribution is about MULTI-STAGE pipelines. Does "own answer first" at Stage 3 affect Stage 4-6 quality?

**Research question**: How should cognitive forcing be distributed across a multi-stage AI pipeline to maximize quality while minimizing friction?

### Track 3: The Friction-Adoption Paradox
**Gap**: Buçinca (2021) found the most effective interventions are rated least favorably. This is the core adoption challenge — if people hate the intervention, they won't use it, and the governance fails.

**Research question**: Can the friction-adoption paradox be resolved through design, or is it a fundamental trade-off?

### Track 4: Minimum Absorptive Capacity Threshold
**Gap**: Cohen and Levinthal (1990) establish that absorptive capacity is the prerequisite for AI adoption, but don't specify the minimum threshold for AI governance specifically.

**Research question**: What is the minimum knowledge an organization needs to implement the 3-layer solution, and can the solution itself provide that knowledge?

### Track 5: Shadow AI Formalization Protocol
**Gap**: 90% of AI tools operate without approval. Waters-Lynch (2025) argues for "integrative" response. But no one has designed a specific protocol for formalizing shadow AI use with minimum viable governance.

**Research question**: What does a minimum viable formalization protocol look like that preserves shadow AI's innovation benefits while adding the 3-layer governance?
