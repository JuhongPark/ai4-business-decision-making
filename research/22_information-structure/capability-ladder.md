# Capability Ladder for Business Decision-Making: OR, LLMs, and Human Judgment

status: draft
phase: 22
date: 2026-03-14

## Research Question

If current LLMs do not fully replace business decision-makers, is that a permanent boundary or a temporary capability gap? More specifically: how should we understand replacement potential when human decision-making itself exists at different levels of sophistication?

## Core Claim

The phrase "LLMs cannot replace business decision-making" is too blunt to be analytically useful.

A better formulation is:

Business decision-making is not one capability. It is a layered stack of capabilities. OR, conventional predictive models, LLMs, and human decision-makers each perform differently at different layers. As model capability improves, replacement does not happen all at once. It moves upward through the stack unevenly.

This means the important research question is not whether AI replaces "the human" in general. It is which layers of human judgment are most vulnerable, which are most complementable, and which remain difficult because they involve unresolved goals, legitimacy, accountability, and organizational power.

## Why "Not Yet" Matters

The user's concern about the word "yet" is well placed.

If we say only that LLMs cannot replace decision-makers "yet," we imply a simple linear story: models improve, then full replacement arrives.

That story is too simple.

Some parts of decision-making are likely to be replaced or heavily compressed.
Some are likely to be persistently hybrid.
Some may remain human-controlled not because of technical weakness alone, but because organizations require legitimacy, responsibility, and governance.

So "yet" should be unpacked into three different claims:

1. capability frontier claim
Some tasks are not replaced because models are not yet good enough.

2. system design claim
Some tasks are not replaced because firms have not yet built reliable sociotechnical systems around the models.

3. institutional claim
Some tasks are not replaced because replacement would conflict with accountability, law, legitimacy, or organizational acceptance even if model quality improved.

## A Capability Ladder

The most useful way to study this is as a ladder of decision capabilities.

### Level 1: Retrieval and basic synthesis

Examples:

- gathering relevant documents
- summarizing reports
- extracting key points
- organizing inputs for review

Replacement potential:
Very high.

Why:
These tasks are language-heavy, repeatable, and often weakly dependent on tacit organizational authority.

Current role of LLMs:
Already strong, though quality control remains necessary.

Human role that remains:
checking accuracy, deciding relevance, spotting missing context

### Level 2: Option expansion and first-pass analysis

Examples:

- generating candidate actions
- identifying plausible pros and cons
- drafting scenarios
- producing initial hypotheses

Replacement potential:
High for first-pass work, lower for final judgment.

Why:
LLMs are good at breadth and speed, but weaker at calibrated commitment and true grounding.

Current role of LLMs:
Strong complements and partial substitutes for junior analytical work.

Human role that remains:
pruning low-quality options, judging realism, deciding which alternatives deserve real consideration

### Level 3: Structured sub-decisions

Examples:

- inventory allocation
- route optimization
- pricing within defined rules
- fraud triage thresholds
- credit scoring components

Replacement potential:
High where objectives and constraints are explicit.

Why:
This is where OR and classical predictive models are strongest, often with LLMs playing only a supporting interface role.

Current role of LLMs:
Useful for interface, explanation, and workflow integration more than for the core optimization itself.

Human role that remains:
setting guardrails, reviewing edge cases, monitoring drift, owning accountability

### Level 4: Problem framing and cross-functional translation

Examples:

- clarifying what the actual decision is
- translating between finance, legal, operations, and product concerns
- surfacing hidden assumptions
- identifying conflicting goals

Replacement potential:
Moderate in assistance terms, lower in authority terms.

Why:
LLMs are increasingly useful here because the work is linguistic and contextual. But this layer also depends on tacit organizational knowledge and political sensitivity.

Current role of LLMs:
Powerful assistants for reframing, summarizing tensions, and revealing tradeoffs.

Human role that remains:
deciding which framing is legitimate, organizationally acceptable, and strategically meaningful

### Level 5: Judgment under ambiguity

Examples:

- choosing under incomplete and conflicting evidence
- deciding when to act despite unclear probabilities
- balancing short-term and long-term tradeoffs
- deciding how much uncertainty the firm should tolerate

Replacement potential:
Limited and uneven.

Why:
These tasks do not just require inference. They require commitment under ambiguity, often without stable objectives or probabilities.

Current role of LLMs:
Useful for surfacing assumptions and stress-testing arguments.

Human role that remains:
bearing uncertainty, choosing despite incompleteness, accepting responsibility for irreversible consequences

### Level 6: Legitimacy, accountability, and political alignment

Examples:

- making a decision acceptable to executives, regulators, and employees
- allocating responsibility
- sequencing change to reduce resistance
- defending the decision publicly or internally

Replacement potential:
Low in pure technical terms, and even lower in institutional terms.

Why:
This level is not only about cognitive performance. It is about authority, legitimacy, and the social acceptance of power.

Current role of LLMs:
supporting communication, argument mapping, and stakeholder analysis

Human role that remains:
authorizing, owning, and justifying the decision in the real organization

### Level 7: Constitutional choice and meta-decision

Examples:

- deciding what the firm's true objective should be
- choosing what not to optimize
- defining escalation rules
- deciding where AI may or may not have authority

Replacement potential:
Very low as an autonomous function.

Why:
This is decision-making about the structure of decision-making itself. It is deeply normative and institutional.

Current role of LLMs:
advisory support, comparison of governance options, scenario exploration

Human role that remains:
setting the constitutional boundaries of authority and responsibility

## OR, LLM, and Human Strength by Level

### OR

Best at:

- level 3 structured sub-decisions
- parts of level 5 when probabilities, objectives, and constraints can be formalized

Weak at:

- levels 4, 6, and 7 unless these are artificially reduced to quantified proxies

### LLMs

Best at:

- levels 1 and 2
- parts of level 4
- support functions at levels 5 and 6

Weak at:

- autonomous authority at levels 5, 6, and 7
- stable accountability and grounded commitment

### Humans

Best at:

- legitimacy-bearing judgment
- constitutional and political choice
- accountability ownership
- acting under unresolved ambiguity

Weak at:

- large-scale retrieval
- exhaustive comparison
- consistency across repetitive judgment tasks

## The Real Meaning of Replacement

Once decision capability is broken into levels, "replacement" has at least four meanings:

1. task replacement
The model performs the same narrow task a human used to do.

2. workflow compression
Fewer humans are needed because the system reduces supporting labor around the decision.

3. authority transfer
The model's output determines action without meaningful human override.

4. institutional substitution
The model is accepted as a legitimate bearer of decision responsibility.

Current LLMs are already achieving forms 1 and 2 in some areas.
They achieve form 3 in bounded environments only with heavy guardrails.
They do not generally achieve form 4.

This distinction matters because many public discussions confuse workflow compression with full decision replacement.

## Research Interpretation

From this perspective, the future is unlikely to be a single event in which "AI replaces managers."

A more realistic pattern is:

- lower-level analytical and linguistic tasks are compressed first
- structured sub-decisions become increasingly automated
- mid-level framing becomes hybrid, with humans supervising machine-generated structure
- high-level legitimacy and constitutional decisions remain human-governed longer

This is not because upper layers are mystical. It is because they combine cognition with power, responsibility, and institutional acceptance.

## Implications for the Earlier Phase 22 Argument

This ladder sharpens the earlier information-structure thesis.

If information access and first-pass synthesis become cheap, scarcity shifts upward.

If LLMs also make problem articulation and option generation cheaper, scarcity shifts upward again.

What remains scarce is not "thinking" in general but a narrower set of capabilities:

- calibrated judgment under ambiguity
- legitimacy-bearing decision ownership
- cross-stakeholder alignment
- design of escalation and governance boundaries

This helps explain why advisory roles may shrink in some layers but persist or even strengthen in others.

## Strong Thesis

LLMs should not be understood as moving business decision-making from human control to machine control in one step. They should be understood as driving an upward migration of scarcity through the layers of decision capability.

## Provisional Conclusion

The right question is not whether LLMs will replace business decision-makers "eventually." The right question is how far up the capability ladder replacement, compression, hybridization, and authority transfer can move.

Current evidence suggests that LLMs are already displacing lower-level cognitive labor, increasingly restructuring mid-level analytical work, and pressuring some expert-intermediary functions. But the highest levels of business decision-making remain difficult not only because of current model quality, but because those levels embed legitimacy, responsibility, and institutional choice.
