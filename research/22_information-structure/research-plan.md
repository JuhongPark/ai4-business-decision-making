# Phase 22 Research Plan: Information-Structure Shocks and Business Decision-Making

## Research Background

The earlier phases of this project focused on AI authority, governance readiness, and organizational conditions for responsible business decision-making. That work treated information quality, evidence, and workflow design as important inputs, but it did not isolate a prior question: how changes in the structure of the information environment reshape decision-making itself.

This phase addresses that gap. It studies major information-structure shocks such as the public internet, search engines, scalable analytics infrastructure, social media feedback systems, and AI-based recommendation or prediction systems. These developments did not matter simply because they were large technologies. They mattered because they changed one or more of the core properties of usable information: acquisition cost, search cost, volume, speed, verifiability, comparability, and interpretive difficulty.

The motivating puzzle is straightforward. As information became cheaper and more widely available, one might expect expert intermediaries such as strategy consultancies to lose value. Yet firms such as BCG, McKinsey, and Bain remained influential and commercially successful. This phase examines whether information democratization reduced the value of information possession while increasing the value of information selection, interpretation, justification, and organizational execution.

## Research Question

primary_question: Why did major reductions in information access costs not eliminate the demand for expert decision intermediaries in business?

## Supporting Questions

1. Which historical developments qualify as true information-structure shocks rather than merely large technology trends?
2. How did each shock change the relative value of information acquisition, selection, interpretation, and execution?
3. Under what conditions does information abundance improve business decision quality, and under what conditions does it create overload, noise, or coordination problems?
4. Why do external advisors remain valuable when firms can independently access more data, benchmarks, and analytical tools?
5. What does this imply for managerial capability design in the AI era?

## Working Argument

The central argument of this phase is that information technology repeatedly compresses information access advantages while increasing the value of higher-order decision capabilities. As information becomes easier to obtain, scarcity shifts upward from possession to selection, interpretation, trust calibration, organizational alignment, and execution. Expert intermediaries persist not because they control raw information, but because they help firms structure meaning and action under conditions of abundance and uncertainty.

## Conceptual Frame

This phase evaluates each candidate shock through seven information variables:

1. acquisition cost
2. search cost
3. information volume
4. information velocity
5. verifiability
6. comparability across sources
7. interpretive burden

The research then maps changes in those variables onto five business decision capabilities:

1. sensing and gathering
2. filtering and prioritizing
3. analysis and interpretation
4. justification and organizational alignment
5. execution and adaptation

## Event Selection Rule

An event or technology wave is included only if it changed the information environment at the system level. Inclusion requires a substantial shift in at least one of the seven information variables above, with clear implications for firm-level decision-making.

This rule centers the phase on the following candidate shocks:

- internet diffusion
- search engine maturation
- enterprise analytics, cloud, and SaaS decision infrastructure
- social media and real-time external feedback loops
- AI-based prediction, recommendation, and summarization systems

It treats other developments such as smartphones primarily as transmission or acceleration mechanisms unless they independently changed decision-relevant information structure.

## Research Design

### Method

This phase uses a structured analytical comparison rather than a claim of causal measurement. It combines:

- conceptual analysis grounded in information economics and organizational decision theory
- comparative historical interpretation of major information-structure shocks
- business-model interpretation using expert intermediaries as a test case
- synthesis into managerial implications for the AI period

### Unit of Analysis

The unit of analysis is the relationship between an information-structure shock and the firm-level decision process.

### Analytical Sequence

1. define the information variables changed by each shock
2. identify which decision capabilities became less valuable
3. identify which decision capabilities became more valuable
4. assess whether the shock reduced or increased the need for expert intermediation
5. compare patterns across shocks
6. derive implications for AI-enabled business decision systems

## Core Propositions

1. Information access improvements reduce the value of proprietary access faster than they reduce the value of interpretation.
2. Information abundance increases the managerial importance of selection, prioritization, and trust calibration.
3. As search and analysis tools diffuse, external advisors shift from information providers toward framing, legitimization, and execution partners.
4. The persistence of consulting demand is evidence that interpretation and organizational alignment remain scarcer than raw information.
5. AI will continue this pattern by compressing some analytical tasks while increasing the value of problem definition, exception handling, and governance.

## Expected Outputs

- a phase-specific research memo defining information-structure shocks
- an analytical comparison of major shocks and their decision effects
- a set of propositions linking information abundance to expert intermediation
- managerial implications for decision capability design in the AI era

## Anticipated Contribution

This phase extends the repository's earlier governance work by shifting one level upstream. Instead of asking only how firms should govern AI in decisions, it asks why decision support remains organizationally difficult even after repeated waves of information democratization. The expected contribution is a cleaner explanation of why better information access does not remove the need for judgment-intensive organizational functions.
