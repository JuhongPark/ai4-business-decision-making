# LLMs and the New Decision Paradigm: From Solving Structured Choices to Structuring Unresolved Choices

status: draft
phase: 22
date: 2026-03-14

## Research Question

How do large language models change the theory and practice of business decision-making when many real organizational choices are not fully representable as classical mathematical decision problems?

## Core Claim

The strongest difference introduced by LLMs is not that they outperform classical mathematical decision theory on the same well-defined optimization problems. It is that they expand decision support into areas where the main difficulty lies upstream of optimization.

Classical models are strongest when:

- options are defined
- objectives are stable
- constraints are observable
- probabilities can be estimated
- payoffs can be compared within a common frame

Many consequential business decisions do not begin in that condition. The hard part is often:

- defining the actual problem
- surfacing hidden objectives and tradeoffs
- synthesizing fragmented qualitative information
- generating plausible alternatives
- translating across stakeholders and functions
- explaining and revising the decision logic as the situation evolves

LLMs matter because they are unusually capable at these pre-optimization and mid-interpretation tasks. They operate less like a closed-form decision rule and more like a language-based system for structuring unresolved choices.

## Why This Is a Real Shift

Traditional decision theory often begins with a formal decision problem. A decision-maker is assumed to face a set of actions, states, probabilities, and payoffs. From there, the question is how to choose rationally under risk or uncertainty.

That tradition remains important. But it leaves a gap between formal theory and actual organizational practice.

Herbert Simon's bounded-rationality program was already a response to that gap. Simon argued that the task was to replace the global rationality of economic man with a form of rationality compatible with real informational and computational limits. In other words, the problem was not merely how to calculate better. It was how to act under limited attention, incomplete information, and finite reasoning capacity.

LLMs can be interpreted as a new layer in that history. They do not eliminate bounded rationality. They alter it. They reduce some of the burdens of searching, summarizing, reframing, drafting, and comparing. In doing so, they push decision support beyond narrow domain-specific tools toward a more general-purpose support technology for open-ended managerial reasoning.

## Link to Existing Theory

### 1. Classical decision theory

Expected utility, Bayesian decision theory, and related mathematical frameworks remain the benchmark for decisions that can be formally represented.

Their strength:

- internally consistent reasoning under uncertainty
- explicit treatment of tradeoffs
- transparent optimization once assumptions are specified

Their limitation in organizational life:

- they often take the problem form as given
- they require clearer objectives than organizations typically possess
- they usually treat implementation and political alignment as external issues

### 2. Bounded rationality

Bounded rationality shifts focus from ideal optimization to feasible reasoning under real constraints.

This is the closest classical bridge to the LLM moment because it recognizes that decision quality depends on information access, attention, and computational limits, not only on formal optimality.

### 3. Behavioral and organizational decision theory

Behavioral theory shows that human judgment is shaped by heuristics, framing, and overload.

Organizational decision theory adds that firms do not merely choose among options. They negotiate goals, allocate attention, create routines, and coordinate action across conflicting interests.

These traditions imply that many business decisions fail before optimization ever begins.

### 4. AI as prediction

Agrawal, Gans, and Goldfarb interpret AI primarily as a prediction technology. Their contribution is critical because it shows that improved prediction does not remove the need for judgment. It changes where judgment is needed.

This framing helps explain the transition from earlier machine-learning systems to LLMs. Conventional predictive AI lowers the cost of forecasting states. LLMs additionally lower some of the costs of language-mediated reasoning, synthesis, option generation, and explanation.

## The New Contribution of LLMs

The most useful way to describe LLMs in this study is not "they solve decision-making." It is:

LLMs help convert messy, weakly structured, and linguistically expressed situations into forms that humans can examine, compare, revise, and sometimes formalize.

That contribution has at least six components:

1. Problem articulation
LLMs can help turn vague concerns into explicit decision questions.

2. Information synthesis
They can aggregate fragmented textual evidence faster than traditional workflows.

3. Alternative generation
They can propose options that decision-makers may not have enumerated.

4. Tradeoff surfacing
They can expose conflicting objectives and stakeholder concerns in language.

5. Translation across functions
They can restate issues in ways legible to finance, legal, product, operations, and leadership audiences.

6. Iterative reframing
They support back-and-forth reformulation, which is central when the first framing is wrong or incomplete.

## This Does Not Mean LLMs "Solve" Everything

This argument needs a clear restraint.

LLMs do not remove the need for formal models where those models are appropriate. They also do not reliably produce correct decisions when simply prompted without structure.

Recent work on DeLLMa is useful here. It argues that directly prompting LLMs on decision-making under uncertainty performs poorly as complexity increases, and that accuracy improves when LLM use is scaffolded with decision-theoretic and utility-theoretic steps. This suggests that LLMs are powerful, but not because they magically replace structure. They are most useful when helping create, revise, and work within structure.

Likewise, recent decision-support research argues that LLMs are more versatile than prior tools precisely because they can assist with many stages of organizational decision-making, including information collection and alternative generation. But that same versatility introduces new design and governance questions.

## Strong Version of the Thesis

The strongest defensible thesis for this phase is:

LLMs extend decision support from the optimization of already-structured choices to the language-based structuring of choices that are not yet fully formed.

This matters because much of actual business decision-making lives in that zone.

## Implications for Business Decision-Making

If this thesis is right, then the major effect of LLMs is not simply better answers. It is a shift in where decision support enters the process.

Before LLMs:

- support tools often entered after the problem had already been formalized
- analytics mainly evaluated predefined alternatives
- predictive models supported narrow sub-decisions

With LLMs:

- support can begin earlier, during problem framing
- qualitative and quantitative inputs can be worked together more easily
- organizations can iterate faster between framing, evidence gathering, alternative generation, and explanation

This does not eliminate human judgment. It changes its role from primary generator of all structure to supervisor, critic, selector, and governor of machine-assisted structure.

## Implications for Theory

This research direction suggests a reinterpretation of decision theory rather than a rejection of it.

Classical mathematical models remain valid for structured decision components.

But the frontier of decision support is shifting toward a broader stack:

1. problem formulation
2. information synthesis
3. alternative generation
4. selective formalization
5. evaluation
6. explanation
7. execution and revision

LLMs are most novel in stages 1, 2, 3, and 6.

That means the key theoretical shift is from treating decision support mainly as a computational tool for choice to treating it as a sociotechnical system for structuring ambiguity.

## Implications for Consulting and Expert Intermediation

This argument also helps explain why LLMs may disrupt some parts of consulting without eliminating the broader advisory function.

LLMs can compress:

- first-pass research
- summarization
- deck drafting
- hypothesis expansion
- option generation

But they do not automatically replace:

- problem selection
- executive persuasion
- political navigation
- accountability allocation
- organizational sequencing
- implementation ownership

So the likely effect is not the elimination of expert intermediaries, but another upward shift in what remains scarce.

## Working Propositions

1. LLMs create the greatest value in business decisions whose main bottleneck is problem structuring rather than numerical optimization.
2. LLMs are complements, not substitutes, for formal models in decisions that contain both structured and unstructured components.
3. The value of human decision-makers shifts toward critique, escalation, legitimacy, and execution when LLMs take on more of the structuring workload.
4. Firms that use LLMs only for answer generation will underperform firms that use them for iterative framing and selective formalization.
5. LLMs are best understood as general-purpose decision support interfaces, not as standalone decision authorities.

## Provisional Conclusion

The arrival of LLMs does not prove that classical decision theory was wrong. It reveals more clearly where that theory was incomplete for real organizational life.

Classical theory excelled at structured choice once the decision problem had been specified. LLMs matter because they help organizations work on the prior question: what exactly is the decision, what are the relevant options and tradeoffs, how should the issue be framed, and which parts of the problem can be formalized at all?

For that reason, the most important contribution of LLMs is not replacing mathematical decision models. It is making previously weakly structured decisions more discussable, more revisable, and in some cases more formally tractable.
