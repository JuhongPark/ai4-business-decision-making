# Doom Narratives About Consulting: Repeated Predictions of Decline

status: draft
phase: 22
date: 2026-03-14

## Purpose

This memo collects recurring media and commentary narratives claiming that consulting firms would be undermined, displaced, or made obsolete by major information and technology shifts. The goal is not to treat all of these articles as equally rigorous. The goal is to document a repeated pattern of prediction and compare it with the industry's continued resilience and reinvention.

## Bottom Line

A recurring media narrative appears across multiple waves of change:

- the internet would disintermediate consultants
- digital platforms would unbundle consulting
- crowdsourcing and marketplaces would commoditize expertise
- AI would automate the analysis layer and trigger a "Kodak moment"

These predictions were often not entirely wrong about pressure on the business model. But they were too strong about collapse. In practice, consulting firms adapted by shifting upward from information access and routine analysis toward implementation, capability building, transformation, and technology-enabled execution.

## Wave 1: Internet and E-Consulting Anxiety

### Fortune, June 12, 2000

Article:
"Old Consultants Never Die: They Just Go 'e'"

Why it matters:
This is one of the clearest mainstream business-media signs that the web was seen as a major shock to consulting. The piece frames the internet as changing consulting fundamentally and asks whether old-line firms can survive against new e-consultants.

Relevant narrative:
The web changed consulting so abruptly that established firms had to recast themselves as internet-native. The article suggests that some firms might cease to be recognizable as traditional consultancies at all.

Use in research:
Good evidence that the internet period already generated a strong "this time is different" story around consulting disruption.

Source:
- Fortune/CNN archive, June 12, 2000: https://money.cnn.com/magazines/fortune/fortune_archive/2000/06/12/281979/index.htm

## Wave 2: Platform and Marketplace Disruption

### Forbes, March 15, 2014

Article:
"Now, It's The Big Time Consultants That Are Getting Disrupted"

Why it matters:
This article explicitly argues that the same digital matching and platform logic that disrupted agencies and labor markets was now coming for highly paid consultants.

Relevant narrative:
Technology platforms can connect buyers and specialized talent more directly, reducing the need for traditional high-overhead consulting structures.

Use in research:
Useful as evidence that digital disintermediation was expected to hit consulting, not just retail or media.

Source:
- Forbes, March 15, 2014: https://www.forbes.com/sites/gregsatell/2014/03/15/now-its-the-big-time-consultants-that-are-getting-disrupted/

### Forbes, June 24, 2014

Article:
"Aussie Startup Expert360, Aims To Disrupt The Consulting Industry"

Why it matters:
This article records a direct startup-era claim that the consulting market was "ripe for disruption" and that technology-driven marketplaces could attack the traditional model.

Relevant narrative:
Instead of buying the hierarchy and staffing model of large firms, clients could buy exactly the expertise they wanted, more flexibly and more cheaply.

Use in research:
This is strong evidence that internet-platform logic produced repeated expectations of consulting disaggregation.

Source:
- Forbes, June 24, 2014: https://www.forbes.com/sites/jlim/2014/06/24/aussie-startup-expert360-aims-to-disrupt-the-consulting-industry/

### Forbes, July 1, 2014

Article:
"Why Consultants Should Be Accountable For Results, Not Recommendations"

Why it matters:
The article explicitly cites Clay Christensen and colleagues as arguing that the consulting industry was "ripe for disruption."

Relevant narrative:
Traditional consultants produce recommendations rather than results, which opens the door to challengers that can deliver more direct and accountable outcomes.

Use in research:
Helpful bridge between disruption theory and dissatisfaction with the classic advisory-only consulting model.

Source:
- Forbes, July 1, 2014: https://www.forbes.com/sites/ronashkenas/2014/07/01/why-consultants-should-be-accountable-for-results-not-recommendations/

## Wave 3: Digital Transformation and Crowdsourcing

### CEOWORLD Magazine, January 30, 2020

Article:
"Traditional Management Consulting Giving Way to Crowdsourcing"

Why it matters:
This is a blunt example of the "traditional consulting is becoming obsolete" thesis.

Relevant narrative:
Crowdsourcing, AI, and data-driven technologies would make traditional consulting structures increasingly outdated while smaller and more technical firms took their place.

Use in research:
Useful not because it is definitive evidence, but because it shows how strongly the obsolescence narrative circulated in business commentary.

Source:
- CEOWORLD, January 30, 2020: https://ceoworld.biz/2020/01/30/traditional-management-consulting-giving-way-to-crowdsourcing/

### Forrester, September 27, 2016

Article:
"Consultancies are adapting to the digital world"

Why it matters:
This is more moderate than the outright doom pieces, but still confirms that digital change was forcing major consulting firms to alter delivery, hiring, and contracting models.

Relevant narrative:
Consulting was not simply stable; it was being reshaped by customer empowerment, digital technology, reusable assets, and pressure against long multi-year projects.

Use in research:
Important as a midpoint source showing that even analysts who did not predict collapse still saw fundamental adaptation pressure.

Source:
- Forrester, September 27, 2016: https://www.forrester.com/blogs/16-09-27-consultancies_are_adapting_to_the_digital_world/

## Wave 4: AI and the Return of the "End of Consulting" Narrative

### Forbes, August 14, 2018

Article:
"Can AI Give Better Advice Than Management Consultants?"

Why it matters:
This is an early AI-era formulation of the question. The argument begins from the claim that internet-era knowledge gaps had already narrowed and AI would make answers cheaper still.

Relevant narrative:
Answers are cheap in the age of the internet, and AI makes them cheaper again. The consultant role therefore faces a direct challenge unless it shifts toward implementation and interpretation.

Use in research:
Very useful because it almost directly anticipates the phase 22 argument.

Source:
- Forbes, August 14, 2018: https://www.forbes.com/sites/theyec/2018/08/14/can-ai-give-better-advice-than-management-consultants/

### Reuters Breakingviews, October 24, 2025

Article:
"AI sets up Kodak moment for global consultants"

Why it matters:
This is one of the strongest recent formulations of the doom narrative. The "Kodak moment" framing implies that AI may do to consulting what digital photography did to Kodak's legacy business model.

Relevant narrative:
If AI can perform analysis, research, and deck-building far more cheaply, the labor-arbitrage and junior-staff pyramid model of consulting is at risk.

Use in research:
This is ideal evidence that the same prediction pattern returned in the generative AI wave.

Source:
- Reuters Breakingviews, October 24, 2025: title and date visible via syndication listing at Webull: https://www.webull.com/news/13736111054240768

### Financial Times, October 2025

Article:
"Consultancies must become software companies to survive AI boom, IBM executive says"

Why it matters:
This is not an outsider attacking consulting from the sidelines. It is an industry executive warning that firms that do not transform may not survive.

Relevant narrative:
Traditional consulting models are exposed unless firms become more software-like and automate delivery.

Use in research:
Useful as evidence that the doom-or-reinvention framing is now coming from inside the broader consulting industry as well.

Source:
- Financial Times, reported five months before March 14, 2026: https://www.ft.com/content/8535fd82-713b-4f53-9849-d0e523e157bc

## Pattern Across These Narratives

Despite differences in timing and technology, the narrative structure repeats:

1. A new information technology sharply lowers the cost of access, search, or analysis.
2. Commentators infer that intermediaries built on knowledge asymmetry will be disintermediated.
3. Consulting is described as overpriced, too hierarchical, too slow, or too recommendation-heavy.
4. New entrants promise direct access to expertise, software, crowds, or AI instead of expensive traditional firms.
5. The established firms do not disappear, but reposition around execution, transformation, software, data, and capability building.

## Research Interpretation

These doom narratives are important not because they all came true, but because they reveal the repeated intuition behind them:

If consulting were primarily in the business of hoarding information, then each major fall in information costs should have reduced the need for major consulting firms.

What actually happened is more complex. The recurring prediction failure suggests that the market value of consulting rests less on information possession than critics assumed. It rests more on:

- problem framing
- translation across functions
- legitimacy and executive persuasion
- implementation support
- change management
- capability transfer

## How To Use This in Phase 22

This memo can support a useful contrast in the final writeup:

- external narrative: technology should make consultants obsolete
- internal consulting narrative: our value lies in execution and transformation
- academic interpretation: information access became cheaper, but meaning, trust, and execution remained scarce

That three-way comparison can become one of the strongest parts of the phase 22 argument.
