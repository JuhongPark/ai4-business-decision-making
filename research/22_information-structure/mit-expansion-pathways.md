# MIT Expansion Pathways for Research on AI Business Decision-Making

status: draft
date: 2026-03-14

## 1. Purpose

This note consolidates exploratory findings on how the current research program on AI-assisted business decision-making could be extended through MIT-linked opportunities, networks, and case-driven collaboration structures.

The goal is not to claim that MIT is the only path. The goal is to clarify why MIT is a plausible expansion site, what kinds of research roles exist there, and how this research could connect to MIT's organizational, labor, and decision-oriented AI ecosystem.

## 2. Why MIT Is Relevant

The current research has already established a governance-centered framework for deciding when firms should use AI to assist, recommend, or automate high-stakes business decisions.

MIT is relevant because its centers and labs often work in the space between:

- AI capability and organizational redesign
- technological change and labor-market adjustment
- decision support and workflow implementation
- applied research and industry collaboration

This makes MIT a strong fit for extending the project from a normative framework into:

- comparative case studies
- workflow-level operationalization
- infrastructure design
- organizational and governance implementation
- empirical and mixed-method validation

## 3. Main MIT Pathways

The MIT-linked pathways identified in this exploration fall into three levels.

### 3.1 Light-touch research entry

These are low-commitment or early-stage entry points that help test fit and build initial connections.

- public events and seminars
- working-group events
- volunteer-style participation in selected research communities
- open mailing lists and center-affiliated seminar series

These matter because they create research visibility without requiring immediate formal affiliation.

### 3.2 Mid-level affiliation pathways

These are more substantial but still exploratory routes.

- visiting student arrangements based on faculty invitation
- project-based collaboration through action-learning or case-based programs
- center-level introductions through working groups or practitioner networks

These pathways are useful if the objective is to translate the existing framework into collaborative pilot research rather than immediately seek a full employment role.

### 3.3 Full research-role pathways

These are formal research positions.

- research assistant
- technical associate
- predoctoral researcher

The most relevant MIT-facing streams for this project are:

- FutureTech
- MIT Sloan Initiative on the Digital Economy
- Work of the Future
- selected economics, labor, and organization research groups

## 4. Networking Logic: How Connection Usually Happens

The MIT pattern is usually not random networking. It is structured networking through research formats.

The typical flow is:

1. identify a center, project, or seminar with thematic fit
2. attend or follow the public-facing event or research stream
3. contact the relevant faculty member, center lead, or research manager with a short research-fit message
4. convert the contact into a concrete unit of collaboration such as a meeting, project contribution, case study, or working-group participation
5. build toward visiting research, RA, or predoc opportunities

In practice, connections emerge through:

- working groups
- seed-grant ecosystems
- case-study programs
- action-learning structures
- seminar communities

This is important for the project because the most realistic path is not immediate appointment, but research-fit accumulation through smaller structured interactions.

## 5. What Kinds of Cases MIT Provides

MIT-linked research ecosystems provide cases in which AI research connects to organizations, labor systems, and implementation practice.

The most useful case types for this project are:

- enterprise case studies on workflow redesign
- labor and organizational change under AI deployment
- consortium-based collaboration between researchers and firms
- action-learning projects translating research questions into organizational problems

These cases matter because they show how a governance framework can move beyond abstract recommendations into observed organizational patterns.

## 6. What It Means to Keep Up With AI Speed

One major challenge in the current research is the speed gap between AI capability change and the pace of academic synthesis or regulatory settlement.

The MIT-style response is not to wait for a stable final answer. It is to create update mechanisms.

The key mechanisms are:

- working groups with recurring interaction
- short-cycle project funding
- case-study pipelines
- translation of findings into teaching and practitioner materials
- industry-linked feedback loops

This aligns closely with the project's adaptive governance extension.

The implication is that this research should evolve through:

- recurring evidence review
- scenario updates
- regular case refresh
- applied feedback from organizational deployments

rather than rely only on one-time static outputs.

## 7. Practical Next Step

The most coherent next move is:

1. position the current work as a governance-and-organization framework for AI authority allocation
2. identify MIT-linked centers with strong thematic fit
3. translate the framework into one or two empirical extensions
4. build contact through seminars, working groups, and concise outreach
5. use those relationships to pursue case collaboration, visiting research, or RA/predoc opportunities

## 8. Bottom Line

This exploration suggests that the project is best expanded not by abandoning its current orientation, but by making it more operational, comparative, and empirically connected.

MIT matters in this context because it offers a setting where:

- organizational research
- AI capability research
- workflow implementation
- labor and management research
- industry collaboration

can be combined into a credible next-stage research program.
