# Literature Scan: Information-Structure Shocks and Decision-Making

status: draft
phase: 22
date: 2026-03-14

## Purpose

This memo extends the phase 22 argument with targeted literature on information abundance, search-cost reduction, data-driven decision-making, organizational complements, and the persistent role of expert intermediaries.

The literature does not usually ask the exact question "why did the internet not kill BCG?" in a single paper. Instead, the evidence is distributed across several adjacent research streams. The strongest interpretation therefore comes from combining those streams carefully.

## Bottom Line

The literature already supports four parts of the argument quite well:

1. digital technologies reduce information and search frictions
2. more data does not automatically improve decisions because overload and interpretation problems remain
3. performance gains from digital tools depend on organizational complements such as management quality, skilled labor, experimentation, and workflow redesign
4. external experts remain useful when firms need framing, legitimacy, implementation support, and knowledge transfer rather than mere access to raw information

The weakest part of the argument is the most specific one: there is limited direct empirical literature tracing the internet-era survival of strategy consultancies to higher-order information functions. That link is still plausible, but it is mostly an inference from adjacent evidence.

## Research Cluster 1: Information Technology Creates Organizational Complements

### Bresnahan, Brynjolfsson, and Hitt (1999; published 2002)

Main contribution:
This is one of the foundational studies showing that information technology does not work as a standalone productivity input. It works with complementary organizational changes.

Why it matters here:
If digital information tools require decentralization, broader responsibilities, and higher skill, then easier access to information does not eliminate managerial judgment. It increases the value of the capabilities needed to use that information effectively.

Key evidence:
The authors find IT use is complementary to new workplace organization and skilled labor, including broader responsibilities and more decentralized decision-making.

Source:
- https://www.nber.org/papers/w7136

## Research Cluster 2: Search and Digital Trace Data Improve Prediction but Shift Value Upstream

### Wu and Brynjolfsson (2015)

Main contribution:
Search-engine data can improve prediction in real markets.

Why it matters here:
Search engines reduce search costs and generate new predictive signals, but the value shifts toward deciding what to measure and how to interpret it.

Key evidence:
Using Google search data improved out-of-sample housing predictions and beat published expert forecasts for future U.S. home sales by 23.6%.

Source:
- https://www.nber.org/books-and-chapters/economic-analysis-digital-economy/future-prediction-how-google-searches-foreshadow-housing-prices-and-sales

### Smith and Brynjolfsson (2001)

Main contribution:
Even when internet tools lower consumer search costs, market behavior still depends on trust and brand.

Why it matters here:
Lower search costs do not remove the role of interpretation and credibility. This is useful as an analogy for firm decision-making as well.

Key evidence:
In internet shopbot settings, brand continued to matter even when price comparison became easier.

Source:
- https://www.nber.org/books-and-chapters/e-commerce/consumer-decision-making-internet-shopbot-brand-still-matters

## Research Cluster 3: Data-Driven Decision-Making Improves Performance, but Adoption Depends on Complements

### Brynjolfsson, Hitt, and Kim (2011)

Main contribution:
Firms using data-driven decision-making perform better on multiple metrics.

Why it matters here:
This is direct evidence for your intuition that information-based decision capability became more important, not less.

Key evidence:
Using survey data on 179 large public firms, the authors report that firms adopting data-driven decision-making had output and productivity about 5-6% higher than expected given their other investments and IT use.

Source:
- https://aisel.aisnet.org/icis2011/proceedings/economicvalueIS/13/

### Brynjolfsson and McElheran (2016)

Main contribution:
Data-driven decision-making diffused rapidly, but not uniformly.

Why it matters here:
If adoption depends on firm size, IT complements, educated workers, and learning, then data access alone is not enough. Organizational readiness matters.

Key evidence:
Using U.S. Census manufacturing data, the authors find DDD adoption nearly tripled from 11% to 30% between 2005 and 2010, with adoption more likely in firms with relevant complements.

Source:
- https://ideas.repec.org/a/aea/aecrev/v106y2016i5p133-39.html

### Brynjolfsson and McElheran (2016 working paper)

Main contribution:
DDD adoption is associated with higher value added, especially for later adopters.

Why it matters here:
This suggests decision quality gains depend on the firm’s ability to turn data into practice rather than on access alone.

Key evidence:
In a representative manufacturing sample of more than 18,000 plants, later DDD adopters show average value added about 3% greater than non-adopters after controls.

Source:
- https://ideas.repec.org/p/cen/wpaper/16-06.html

## Research Cluster 4: More Information Can Lower Decision Quality Through Overload

### Eppler and Mengis (2004 tradition), recent review by Bawden et al.-linked scoping synthesis (2024)

Main contribution:
Information overload is now well established as a cross-domain problem.

Why it matters here:
This directly supports your argument that more information does not automatically produce better decisions.

Key evidence:
The 2024 scoping review reports that the exponential growth of digital information is linked to poor decision-making, decreased productivity, and cognitive pressure, and that mitigation requires filtering, prioritizing, and supporting technologies.

Source:
- https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2667096824000508

### Iselin (1990)

Main contribution:
Decision quality can decline when information load rises.

Why it matters here:
This is classic experimental evidence that overload can degrade judgment rather than improve it.

Key evidence:
In a financial distress task, about one-third of participants appeared to experience information overload and reached lower-quality decisions.

Source:
- https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/036136829090033Q

## Research Cluster 5: Management Quality and External Intervention Matter

### Bloom et al. (2011; published later)

Main contribution:
Consulting-based management interventions can causally improve firm performance.

Why it matters here:
This is highly relevant to the question of why external experts do not disappear. Even when information and modern practices are in principle available, firms may still need outside help to adopt and use them.

Key evidence:
In Indian textile plants, free consulting raised productivity by 11%, increased decentralization of decision-making, and increased computer use.

Source:
- https://www.nber.org/papers/w16658

### Bloom et al. (2017)

Main contribution:
Management quality explains a meaningful share of productivity dispersion.

Why it matters here:
If management variation matters as much or more than raw IT investment, then the scarce asset is not just technology or information access. It is the quality of managerial interpretation and organizational use.

Key evidence:
Using data from 32,000 U.S. manufacturing plants, the authors report that management variation accounts for about one-fifth of the productivity spread and about twice as much as explained by IT.

Source:
- https://www.nber.org/papers/w23300

### Bloom et al. (2018/2020 follow-up)

Main contribution:
Some consulting gains persist, but diffusion is uneven.

Why it matters here:
This suggests that knowledge transfer and organizational embedding are difficult, which helps explain why outside advisors can remain valuable even after initial information transfer.

Source:
- https://www.nber.org/papers/w24249

## Research Cluster 6: Experimentation Lowers the Cost of Learning but Still Rewards Capable Managers

### Koning, Hasan, and Chatterji (2019)

Main contribution:
A/B testing reduces the cost of experimentation and can improve performance.

Why it matters here:
This fits the broader pattern: digital tools lower learning costs, but not every firm adopts them, and experienced managers benefit more.

Key evidence:
Among startups that adopted A/B testing, the authors find improved performance on page views and new product features, with stronger gains for firms with experienced managers.

Source:
- https://www.nber.org/papers/w26278

## Research Cluster 7: Newer Digital Infrastructure Still Requires Long Learning Periods

### Brand, Demirer, Finucane, and Kreps (2024; revised 2025)

Main contribution:
Firms take time to learn how to use digital infrastructure productively.

Why it matters here:
This supports the idea that technology adoption does not erase organizational judgment problems. Learning and internal capability remain major bottlenecks.

Key evidence:
Using cloud-computing data from nearly 100,000 firms, the authors find firms improve cloud productivity by 33% in the first year, reach stability only after four years, and show minimal knowledge transfer across divisions.

Source:
- https://www.nber.org/papers/w32938

## Research Cluster 8: Generative AI Changes Team Production but Does Not Eliminate Organizational Design Problems

### Dell'Acqua et al. (2025)

Main contribution:
Generative AI can replicate part of the performance benefit of teamwork in knowledge work.

Why it matters here:
AI reduces some information-processing burdens, but it changes rather than removes the need for organizational design. It affects how expertise is combined, how teams are structured, and where human coordination remains necessary.

Key evidence:
In a field experiment with 776 professionals at Procter & Gamble, individuals using AI matched the performance of teams without AI, while also producing more balanced cross-functional solutions.

Source:
- https://www.nber.org/papers/w33641

## Research Cluster 9: Why External Experts Persist

### Hargadon (1998)

Main contribution:
Knowledge brokers create value by transferring and recombining ideas across settings.

Why it matters here:
This is one of the clearest conceptual arguments for why firms such as consultancies can persist in information-rich environments. Their value is not owning facts, but recombining ideas and translating knowledge across contexts.

Key evidence:
The article argues that some firms create value by spanning industries and moving knowledge from where it is known to where it is not, often in consulting-like roles.

Source:
- https://cmr.berkeley.edu/1998/05/40-3-firms-as-knowledge-brokers-lessons-in-pursuing-continuous-innovation/

### Howlett and Migone (2013)

Main contribution:
The literature on external consultants identifies multiple reasons organizations buy outside advice.

Why it matters here:
This is one of the best sources for the non-information reasons consultants survive: technical skills, symbolic impression management, legitimacy, and change management.

Key evidence:
The paper summarizes private-sector explanations for consultant use in three categories: needed technical skills, symbolic impression management, and legitimization/change management.

Source:
- https://academic.oup.com/policyandsociety/article-abstract/32/3/241/6420753

### Bortz (2023)

Main contribution:
Consultant influence varies with openness, trust, and discursive fit.

Why it matters here:
This reinforces the point that consultants matter not only because they know things, but because their advice resonates politically and organizationally.

Source:
- https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11077-023-09506-5

## What This Literature Now Lets Us Claim with Confidence

High confidence:

- Digital information technologies create performance gains mainly when combined with organizational complements.
- Data-rich environments raise the value of filtering, interpretation, and management quality.
- Information overload is a real and recurring problem in organizational decision-making.
- External interventions such as consulting can improve managerial practice and decision structures.
- The role of outside experts includes legitimacy, framing, and implementation support, not only information transfer.

Moderate confidence:

- The survival of strategy consulting after the internet is consistent with a shift from information scarcity toward meaning scarcity and execution scarcity.
- AI is likely to continue this pattern by lowering some analytical costs while increasing the importance of problem definition and governance.

Lower confidence or open question:

- There is still no single decisive empirical study showing that BCG-like firms survived specifically because information democratization increased the value of interpretation and organizational alignment.
- The strongest version of that claim still needs either direct industry evidence or a more formal historical analysis of consulting business models.

## Best Next Research Moves

1. Add a focused review on management consulting as knowledge brokering and legitimation.
2. Add industry evidence on the growth and repositioning of major consulting firms after the internet and after enterprise analytics diffusion.
3. Build a table that maps each information shock to what became cheaper and what became scarcer.
4. Separate claims with direct causal support from interpretive synthesis claims.

## Working Interpretation for Phase 22

The accumulated literature supports a refined version of the phase 22 thesis:

Digital technologies repeatedly reduce the cost of accessing, searching, storing, and processing information. But they do not proportionally reduce the cost of identifying what matters, trusting what is true, aligning stakeholders around a decision, and embedding that decision in organizational routines. As a result, information abundance can increase the value of higher-order managerial and advisory functions even while reducing the value of raw information possession.
