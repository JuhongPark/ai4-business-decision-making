# Research Note Synthesis: Information Shocks, Consulting Resilience, and LLM-Era Decision-Making

status: draft
date: 2026-03-14

## 1. Starting Question

This research began from a practical puzzle.

Major information shocks such as the public internet, search engines, cloud analytics, and now LLMs sharply reduce the cost of finding, processing, and distributing information. If that is true, why do organizations still rely on expert intermediaries such as consulting firms? And why does business decision-making remain difficult even when so much more information is available?

## 2. Core Argument

The central answer is that these technologies reduce the scarcity of information access faster than they reduce the scarcity of interpretation, coordination, legitimacy, and execution.

In earlier periods, advantage often came from obtaining hard-to-find information.

In the modern information environment, advantage increasingly comes from:

- deciding what matters
- framing the actual problem
- translating across functions and stakeholders
- choosing under ambiguity
- creating organizational legitimacy for a choice
- turning analysis into coordinated execution

This means information democratization does not remove expertise. It shifts the layer at which expertise remains scarce.

## 3. Which Historical Events Actually Matter

Not every major technology trend matters equally for this argument. The relevant events are those that changed the structure of decision-relevant information itself.

The most important events are:

- public internet diffusion
- search engine maturation
- enterprise analytics, cloud, and SaaS decision infrastructure
- social media and real-time feedback systems
- AI systems for prediction, recommendation, summarization, and language-based reasoning

These are important because they changed one or more of the following:

- acquisition cost
- search cost
- information volume
- information velocity
- verifiability
- comparability
- interpretive burden

## 4. What Changed in Business Decision-Making

Across these information shocks, the pattern is consistent.

What became cheaper:

- basic information access
- routine retrieval
- first-pass aggregation
- surface benchmarking

What became more difficult or more valuable:

- filtering signal from noise
- defining the real decision problem
- managing information overload
- integrating qualitative and quantitative inputs
- aligning multiple stakeholders
- executing under uncertainty

The bottleneck moved from access to attention, then from attention to interpretation, and then from interpretation to organizational execution.

## 5. Why Consulting Firms Did Not Disappear

Repeated media narratives predicted that consulting would be weakened or even made obsolete by the internet, digital platforms, crowdsourcing, and later AI.

Those narratives were directionally right that the old consulting model would come under pressure. But they were too strong in predicting collapse.

The industry adapted by moving upward in the value stack.

Consulting firms increasingly positioned themselves not as holders of privileged information, but as providers of:

- problem framing
- transformation design
- capability building
- change management
- implementation support
- AI scaling and value realization

Official narratives from BCG, McKinsey, and Bain are strikingly consistent on this point. They rarely emphasize exclusive information access. They emphasize execution, transformation, operating-model redesign, and value capture.

## 6. What the Broader Literature Supports

The literature strongly supports several parts of this argument.

It supports that:

- digital technologies create value when paired with organizational complements
- more information does not automatically improve decisions because overload and interpretation problems remain
- management quality and workflow design matter for turning data into performance
- external experts can create value through knowledge transfer, framing, legitimacy, and implementation support

The literature is weaker on one narrow claim: there is limited direct empirical work showing that the internet failed to destroy consulting specifically because higher-order interpretation and execution remained scarce. That claim is best treated as a synthesis built from adjacent evidence rather than as a single directly proven result.

## 7. Relation to Classical Decision Theory

This research does not reject classical decision theory. It repositions it.

Classical decision theory, OR, expected utility, and Bayesian models are powerful when:

- the decision problem is already defined
- objectives are relatively stable
- constraints are known
- uncertainty can be represented
- outcomes are comparable

The problem is that many consequential business decisions do not begin in that state.

The hardest part is often upstream:

- What exactly is the decision?
- What should the organization optimize?
- Which constraints really matter?
- Which tradeoffs are politically and ethically acceptable?
- Which parts of the issue can be formalized at all?

So the real limitation is not that mathematical modeling is useless. It is that many business problems are only partially formalizable, and the act of formalization itself leaves out important parts of organizational reality.

## 8. What LLMs Change

LLMs matter because they are useful in precisely the layers where many business decisions are weakly structured.

They are not best understood as universal decision engines.
They are best understood as language-based systems for:

- articulating problems
- synthesizing fragmented information
- generating alternatives
- surfacing tradeoffs
- translating across functions
- iteratively reframing the issue

This makes them especially important in the gap between raw information and fully formalized decision models.

The strongest claim is therefore:

LLMs extend decision support from solving already-structured choices to helping structure choices that are not yet fully formed.

## 9. Why LLMs Still Do Not Fully Replace Business Decision-Makers

The answer is not just "quality."

Quality matters. LLMs can hallucinate, misread context, and produce unstable outputs.

But the larger reason is that business decisions are not simply answer-generation tasks. They also involve:

- goal selection
- responsibility allocation
- political alignment
- legitimacy
- escalation rules
- execution ownership

These are not just cognitive tasks. They are organizational and institutional tasks.

So the more precise statement is:

Current LLMs do not fully replace business decision-makers because business decisions combine cognition with legitimacy, accountability, and implementation.

## 10. A Capability Ladder View

The most useful way to think about replacement is not as a yes-or-no question.

Business decision-making is a ladder of capabilities.

Lower levels:

- retrieval
- summarization
- first-pass analysis
- option expansion

These are already highly compressible by LLMs.

Middle levels:

- problem framing
- cross-functional translation
- tradeoff surfacing

These are increasingly hybrid, with LLMs assisting and humans supervising.

Upper levels:

- judgment under ambiguity
- legitimacy-bearing choice
- accountability ownership
- meta-decision about authority and governance

These remain much harder to replace, not only because of model quality, but because they embed institutional responsibility.

This leads to a better interpretation of "not yet."

Some tasks are not yet replaceable because the models are still not good enough.
Some are not yet replaceable because firms lack the systems around them.
Some may remain human-controlled because organizations require legitimate human authority even if technical capability improves.

## 11. Why Organizations Often React Slowly Even When Experts Exist

Another important conclusion is that firms often do contain people who see technological and market shifts early.

The problem is usually not pure ignorance.

More often, firms struggle because:

- early signals are hard to translate into business priorities
- technical language does not map cleanly onto finance, operations, or risk language
- experts lack authority or coalition support
- existing incentives reward stability over early adaptation
- acting on the new signal threatens incumbent power structures

This helps explain why firms often hire consultants even when internal experts already understand the issue. Consultants can act as translators, legitimizers, and organizers of decision momentum.

## 12. Overall Synthesis

Taken together, the research suggests the following broad interpretation.

Modern information technologies repeatedly make information cheaper, faster, and more available. Each time, observers predict that expert intermediaries will disappear and that decision-making will become easier. But in practice, the bottleneck moves upward.

First the scarce resource was access.
Then it became attention.
Then interpretation.
Now, increasingly, the scarce resource is legitimate and executable judgment in complex organizations.

LLMs accelerate this trend. They compress more of the lower and middle layers of information work. But that does not automatically eliminate human decision-makers. It pushes scarcity further upward toward ambiguity-bearing judgment, institutional legitimacy, governance design, and execution responsibility.

## 13. Working Thesis

The best summary thesis at this stage is:

Digital information shocks and LLMs do not eliminate the need for expert judgment. They repeatedly reduce the cost of lower-order information work and thereby raise the relative importance of higher-order capabilities: problem definition, interpretation, legitimacy, coordination, and execution.

## 14. Open Questions for Further Work

The strongest next questions are:

1. How far up the decision capability ladder can LLM-supported systems move in practice?
2. Which industries will transfer authority faster, and which will preserve human control longer?
3. Under what conditions do internal experts fail to move organizations despite seeing the signal early?
4. When do consulting firms add real decision value, and when do they mainly add legitimacy?
5. Can LLM systems become accepted as legitimate decision authorities, or will they remain decision-support tools even as quality improves?

## 15. Domain Layering

The same argument looks different across organizational settings.

In large firms, the upper layer of scarce judgment is executable strategic choice under organizational complexity.
In government, it is legitimate public authority.
In startups, it is directional commitment under radical uncertainty.
In academia, it is epistemic judgment about what counts as valid knowledge.

This means the question "will LLMs replace decision-makers?" cannot be answered in the abstract. It must be answered at the level of domain, because each domain has a different objective structure, accountability regime, and upper boundary of legitimate delegation.

The contrast between large firms and startups is especially important. Large firms struggle most with coordination, legacy structures, and internal legitimacy across multiple stakeholders. Startups struggle most with directional choice, speed, survival, and commitment under weak evidence. So even within "business," the upper layer of scarce judgment differs substantially.
