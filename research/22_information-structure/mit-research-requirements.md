# MIT Research Requirements for AI Business Decision-Making Extension

status: draft
date: 2026-03-14

## 1. Purpose

This note organizes the capabilities and methodological orientations that seem most relevant for extending the current AI business decision-making research into MIT-linked research settings.

The focus is not on generic academic preparation. The focus is on what kinds of skills make the project legible and credible in organizational, labor, and technology-adjacent research environments.

## 2. Two Main Research Tracks

The project can be extended through two partially distinct but complementary tracks.

### 2.1 Technical track

The technical track asks:

- how well does the system perform?
- under what conditions does it fail?
- how can authority transitions be measured?
- what infrastructure is needed for monitoring and rollback?

Typical components include:

- model evaluation
- data quality assessment
- drift detection
- simulation
- quantitative modeling
- monitoring and audit infrastructure
- measurable escalation and fallback criteria

This track becomes important when the framework needs operational thresholds and validation logic.

### 2.2 Non-technical track

The non-technical track asks:

- how much authority should AI receive?
- who remains accountable?
- how should governance be structured?
- how do organizations preserve legitimacy and oversight?

Typical components include:

- case studies
- comparative institutional analysis
- organizational design
- human-AI role allocation
- stakeholder legitimacy
- authority and escalation rules

This track remains the current project's main strength.

### 2.3 Practical interpretation

The most coherent extension is not to switch entirely into technical research.

The better path is:

- keep a non-technical core focused on judgment, governance, and authority
- add technical support for measurement, thresholds, monitoring, and validation

## 3. Core Research Skills

The exploration suggests that MIT-linked opportunities in this area require a blend of research, quantitative, and communication skills.

### 3.1 Research skills

- literature synthesis
- research memo writing
- problem formulation
- qualitative and mixed-method reasoning
- structured comparison across cases and institutions

### 3.2 Quantitative and programming skills

- Python or R
- data handling and visualization
- statistics
- econometric reasoning
- basic modeling capacity

### 3.3 Communication and collaboration skills

- concise faculty outreach
- clear research positioning
- project coordination
- ability to work across technical and managerial language

## 4. Mathematics Requirements

Mathematics is relevant, but not mainly in the form of advanced pure mathematics.

The more relevant foundation is:

- probability
- statistics
- regression logic
- basic linear algebra
- quantitative reasoning for evaluation

For this project, the important issue is whether arguments can be translated into measurable conditions, comparative evidence, and model-supported claims.

## 5. Is Modeling Needed?

Yes, but mostly in an applied form.

The most relevant forms are:

- empirical modeling
- econometric modeling
- scenario modeling
- workflow modeling
- simulation of escalation and fallback logic

Modeling is useful because it can turn the framework from:

- conceptual distinction

into:

- testable transition conditions
- comparative scenario logic
- measurable governance thresholds

## 6. Research Implication

The project should not be extended as a purely technical AI study or as a purely legal-policy study.

Its strongest future direction is a hybrid path:

- keep the core focus on organizational judgment, governance, and authority allocation
- add selective technical and quantitative tools for measurement, thresholds, and validation
- use methodological pluralism rather than assume one method is sufficient

## 7. Bottom Line

The project is already strong in conceptual framing and governance reasoning.

What it needs next is not a full identity shift, but reinforcement through:

- stronger empirical design
- clearer measurement logic
- modest but credible modeling capacity
- practical research communication suited to collaborative environments
