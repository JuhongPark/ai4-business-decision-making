# Consulting-Firm Narratives: How Major Firms Explain Their Continuing Value

status: draft
phase: 22
date: 2026-03-14

## Purpose

This memo summarizes how major consulting firms describe their own value proposition in the internet and AI era. The goal is not to treat firm marketing as neutral evidence. The goal is to capture the self-narratives that these firms use to justify why they still matter when information is widely accessible.

## Bottom Line

Across BCG, McKinsey, and Bain, the dominant narrative is not "we have information you cannot find." The dominant narrative is:

- we help clients turn strategy into execution
- we help organizations scale technology and AI into measurable value
- we build capabilities, operating models, and governance
- we align leadership and orchestrate change across complex organizations

This is highly consistent with the phase 22 argument that information access has become less scarce while interpretation, coordination, and implementation remain scarce.

## BCG Narrative

### Core Positioning

BCG increasingly describes its value in terms of value creation from AI, enterprise transformation, capability building, and scaling adoption across the organization.

### Main Themes

1. Adoption and value creation matter more than technology alone.
2. AI transformation requires organization and workforce change, not just models.
3. Capability building is central to enterprise-wide transformation.
4. Firms struggle to scale AI value because people and process issues dominate technology issues.

### Evidence from Official Sources

- On September 23, 2025, BCG announced a joint AI research partnership with TUM and explicitly framed the key challenge as not only technological leadership but also "adoption and value creation through AI in companies."
- On September 15, 2025, BCG highlighted IDC MarketScape language describing BCG as combining strategic, functional, industry, organization and process change, and technology expertise to help clients deliver value at scale from AI.
- On October 24, 2024, BCG stated that 74% of companies struggle to achieve and scale value from AI and argued that roughly 70% of scaling barriers come from people and process issues, 20% from technology, and 10% from algorithms.
- In its BCG U capability-building positioning page, BCG says that enterprise transformation requires more than strategy and depends on scalable upskilling and enablement.
- In its February 4, 2026 article on workforce transformation, BCG argues that AI value comes mostly from people, process, and implementation layers rather than algorithms alone.

### Interpretation

BCG's self-narrative is clear: its relevance does not rest on information scarcity. It rests on helping firms convert technological possibility into organizationally realized value.

## McKinsey Narrative

### Core Positioning

McKinsey describes digital and AI work as organizational rewiring and frames its implementation practice around turning recommendations into results.

### Main Themes

1. Digital transformation is a continuous organizational rewiring process.
2. Strategy alone is insufficient without implementation capability.
3. The firm's role is to diagnose execution barriers, build capabilities, and sustain change.
4. Different expertise is needed across diagnosis, design, implementation, and capability building.

### Evidence from Official Sources

- In its implementation practice page, McKinsey states "From recommendations to results" and says its consultants help clients from project initiation through execution and capability building.
- The same page emphasizes execution readiness, bottom-up planning, capability building, and sustaining change after project launch.
- In an August 7, 2024 explainer, McKinsey defines digital transformation as the "rewiring of an organization" to create value by continuously deploying technology at scale.

### Interpretation

McKinsey's narrative is explicitly post-informational. The firm presents its continuing value as execution design, organizational rewiring, and capability building, not privileged access to facts.

## Bain Narrative

### Core Positioning

Bain emphasizes transformation, Results Delivery®, leadership alignment, change management, and AI implementation tied to concrete business outcomes.

### Main Themes

1. Most transformations fail without strong change execution.
2. Bain's value lies in managing transformation risk and making implementation work.
3. AI transformation requires process change, workforce development, operating-model redesign, and technology foundations.
4. Bain positions its proprietary tools as a way to make change measurable, trackable, and durable.

### Evidence from Official Sources

- Bain's Results Delivery® materials argue that traditional PMO-style control is insufficient and instead emphasize coaching, capability building, leadership teaming, and line-led execution.
- Bain's Full Potential Transformation page highlights clear ambition, leadership alignment, talent and capability building, management systems, and Results Delivery® as core elements.
- In its expanded OpenAI partnership announcement on January 16, 2024, Bain said its AI transformation work covers strategy development, process change, workforce and organizational development, and technology foundations.
- Bain's ARC transformation tool page positions the firm as helping clients turn strategy into execution through value tracking, governance, and AI-powered reporting.

### Interpretation

Bain's story is that the difficult part is not discovering best practices. The difficult part is mobilizing people, governing change, and delivering promised value in real organizations.

## Cross-Firm Comparison

The three firms use different language, but their narratives converge strongly.

### Shared Narrative

- value comes from implementation, not just analysis
- clients need capability building, not just advice
- organization design and change management are central
- AI raises the importance of scaling, governance, and workflow redesign
- technology alone rarely produces results

### What They Rarely Claim

- that their core value is exclusive access to raw information
- that internet search or digital tools have made their role obsolete
- that models or algorithms alone create transformation value

## Research Usefulness

These materials are useful as evidence of how the firms want to be understood by the market. They are not neutral proof that the claims are true. But they are highly relevant for phase 22 because they show that the industry's own legitimating narrative has shifted upward from information access toward:

- meaning and framing
- organizational change
- implementation support
- capability building
- measurable value realization

## Implications for Phase 22

The consulting-firm narrative aligns closely with the academic interpretation already developing in this phase. If these firms were surviving mainly by guarding access to information, their official self-description would likely emphasize proprietary insight and privileged data access. Instead, they repeatedly emphasize execution, transformation, change, capability, adoption, and value capture.

That does not prove the narrative is fully true. But it does show that the industry's market-facing identity is now built around the claim that the scarce resource is not information itself. The scarce resource is the ability to convert information and technology into coordinated organizational action.
